Historical Scenarios

This module discusses various approaches for generating useful historical stress scenarios.

The most common practice is to use recent historical scenarios (i.e., 5 to 10 years) where data quality is reasonable.

Going back farther often creates challenges of filling in missing datapoints. For example, if the 1987 equity crash happened again today, how would interest rate swap curves move, for which data was not available back then? CDS spreads, which did not exist then (or even the EUR). We discuss the use of Predictive Stress Testing (see Paul Kupiec 1998) to fill in missing data points, as well as the challenges this methodology presents.

We also discuss the use of archetypal historical scenarios, such as common deleveraging trajectories as analyzed by Ken Rogoff and MGI (optional document available here).

We finally discuss the use of Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) scenarios.

Discussion: Pros and Cons of Objective (Historical) Risk Metrics

Objective approaches to risk management have strengths and pitfalls. Scientific measurement provides clarity and precision. But relying exclusively on metrics for real world problems is dangerous because qualitative dimensions are missing. Objective perspectives will underestimate risk because they ignore Black Swans (i.e., events that have no historical precedence). Purely objective approaches to risk management can lead to a number of psychological downfalls. The first is overconfidence (“we know our risks”), and the tendency to over-optimize systems which Nassim Taleb focuses on in “Anti-Fragile” (2012). Furthermore, a focus on quantitative measures often results in overfocus and missing new information (see The Invisible Gorilla experiment)
