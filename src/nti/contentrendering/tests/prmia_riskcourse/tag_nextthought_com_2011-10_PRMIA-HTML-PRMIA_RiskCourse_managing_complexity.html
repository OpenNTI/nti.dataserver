Managing Complexity

Complexity is a function of multiplicity, interdependence, and diversity (see G. Sargut & R.G. Mcgrath, HBR Sept 2011).

Complex systems are hard to predict and difficult to manage, posing significant perceptual and cognitive challenges. There is constant tension between amplifying and dampening feedback mechanisms, and their delayed effects and non-linear interaction. As discussed in our first module, the amplifying feedback between asset prices and leverage is one of the central drivers of systemic risk.

A multiplicity of interacting elements can cause unintended consequences that can spiral in a butterfly effect, which unchecked could lead to the failure of systems to adapt.

Tim Harford’s Adapt: Why Success Always Starts With Failure gives some excellent advice on navigating complexity.

  • Recognize that “we are blinder than we think” due to perceptual limitations and cognitive biases.

  • Make failure survivable (decouple systems, small failures).

  • Learn from trial and error (“the art of success is to fail productively”).

Complexity is driving a paradigm shift from “Predict-and-Control” to “Sense-and-Respond”, which is marked by the following attributes:

  • Ability to cope with interdependence and ambiguity

  • Skillful management of polarities

  • Dynamic Steering with real time data and feedback

  • Prioritizing focus on emergent properties
