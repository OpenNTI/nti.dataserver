Adaptive Stress Testing

This module starts with a review of the Adaptive Stress Testing framework (which we first introduced in the 1. Macro Stress Testing module). The core idea of Adaptive Stress Testing is to maximize learning feedback, very much like the trial and error evolutionary process of adaptation to new environments.

Implications of Complexity

It’s futile to make long-term predictions in evolutionary systems. We must be open to emergence and quickly adapt to new conditions. Hence the paradigm shift from “Predict and Control” to “Sense and Respond.” Just as in riding a bicycle, we apply “dynamic steering” to continually adjust course with real time data. Furthermore, due to rapid phase transitions, we must respond to early warning signals in order to exert control during a limited window of opportunity.

Understanding Fault Lines

We begin with an understanding of systemic fault lines, which represents structural risks in potential and invisible form. Examples include leverage, asset valuations, risk aversion, and economic imbalances. We can then analyze portfolios relative to these systemic risks, and better interpret the significance of tremors on key fault lines.

Beware of Disaster Myopia

One of the challenges highlighted by Andrew Haldane’s “Why banks failed the stress test” is disaster myopia, or “propensity to underestimate the probability of adverse outcomes, in particular small probability events from the distant past.” A classic example of disaster myopia was the placement of the Fukishima Reactor in a tsunami and earthquake hot spot.
