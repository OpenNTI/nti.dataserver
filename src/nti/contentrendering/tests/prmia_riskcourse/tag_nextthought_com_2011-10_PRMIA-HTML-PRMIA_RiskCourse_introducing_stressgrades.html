Introducing StressGrades™

StressGrades integrates market intelligence into the stress testing process, and helps focus attention on emerging stress scenarios while they are still manageable.

The idea for StressGrades arose from a discussion with a risk manager who noted that her bank ran over 180 scenarios against hedge fund counterparties on a daily basis, but did not know what to do with all this data.

StressGrades are designed to help risk managers focus on escalating risk signals:

  1. First focus attention on the greatest immediate threats (i.e., high and escalating visible risk)

  2. Then consider emerging scenarios which have high loss potential (i.e., low visible risk, but escalating)

StressGrades builds on the classic Black-Scholes option pricing model. We start by defining a key loss threshold (e.g., −9.6% daily loss for the S&P 500), and then derive a market implied probability of stress (PStress) based observed volatilities. We currently use a Normal distribution and the RiskMetrics methodology for daily volatility forecasting. Other distributionals (e.g., student-t) and volatility forecasting methodologies can be used (e.g., GARCH). Note, however, that our emphasis is less on precision than developing a practical model to detect escalating stress themes. As such the application of a Normal distribution is practical and appropriate.

Backtesting StressGrades: S&P 500 Case Study

To test whether StressGrades has provided useful early warning signals, we ran the following backtest. We picked systemically important ETFs representing multiple asset classes and set the threshold loss level to the worst case actual daily loss experienced during the 2007-2009 financial crisis. For example, in the case of S&P 500 Index (or SPY ETF), log return was −9.6% on Dec 1 2008.

We observed the first early warning signal when equity volatility spiked globally on Feb 27 2007. Over the next 20 months, we saw the classic patterns of endogenously amplified risks, as ever larger tsunamis of volatility pummeled markets. Prior to Feb 27 2007, a −9.6% log return in the S&P 500 would have represented a 24 standard deviation event, but by Dec 1 2008, it was only a 2 standard deviation event.

Backtesting StressGrades™: ETFs

Next we wanted to explore early warning patterns for other systemic stresses. In the slide below we show ETF’s representing the following themes: Financials (XLF), Inflation (GLD), Deflation (10Y), Europe (EZU), China (FXI), US (SPY).

As we saw in our previous S&P 500 case study, we can see a super-exponential escalation of PStress across all assets from Feb 27 2007 until the peak of the crisis in Dec 2008. We can also see a clear progression of stress themes emerge:

  1. Feb 2007, spike in Financials and broader equity markets

  2. June 2007, spike in Deflation theme (as measured by US 10 government bonds)

  3. Aug 2007, spike in Inflation theme

Also noteworthy:

  1. PStress declines in unison across all asset classes after the Dec 2008 spike, indicating broadly declining system risk levels.

  2. Europe PStress surpassed other themes for the first time in Aug 2009. We start to see classic endogenous amplification patterns from October 2009 as the European Sovereign Crisis emerged.

In summary, StressGrades are a methodology for amplifying market intelligence. Instead of being distracted by the daily noise in markets, we focus our attention on outliers (i.e., super-exponential rates of change). StressGrades allows visualization and detection of significant changes in market volatility (i.e., it is a seismograph to detect tremors in financial fault lines).

StressQ (StressQuantile)

StressQ shows the StressGrade percentile rank volatility of a theme, relative to recent history. For example, a StressQ of 95 implies that StressGrades have historically exceeded this level only 5% of the time. The standard time frame for StressQ is 1 year, but other horizons can be used.

As the slide below shows, declining StressQ (i.e., declining volatility) is associated with bull markets, while increasing StressQ is associated with bear markets.

StressQ Can Help Prioritize Visible vs Hidden Risk

Assets with high volatility require immediate attention. The first priority of risk managers is to understand why volatilities are elevated and what could cause it to spike further, and whether this poses immediate threat to the portfolio.

A common mistake, however, is to ignore assets with low volatility. A low StressQ indicates that volatility is low, but as we know volatility only measures visible risk, not potential structural risk. A low StressQ should therefore encourage risk managers to probe for hidden risks which might emerge in the future.

Case Study: Corporate Bonds, A New Safe Haven?

“The world’s biggest fixed-income investors, fed up with yields on benchmark government bonds that pay less than zero percent, say they’ve found a new haven from turmoil sweeping global markets…. The Bank of America Merrill Lynch Global Broad Market Corporate Index, which tracks 9,542 debentures, is on pace to gain 3.61 percent in July, the most since being created in 1997, and 14 percent for the year including reinvested interest…. Even though the global economy is slowing and credit-rating downgrades exceed upgrades, rising demand has allowed companies worldwide to sell about $2.16 trillion of bonds this year, the second-fastest pace on record.” – Bloomberg.com, Investors seek refuge in corporate bonds

Corporate bond ETF’s (from LQD to JNK) currently exhibit low volatility (high Dstress, low PStress, low StressQ), and have been in a bull market since 2009.
