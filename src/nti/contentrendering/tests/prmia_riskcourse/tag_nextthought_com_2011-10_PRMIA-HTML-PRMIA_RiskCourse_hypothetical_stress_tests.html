Hypothetical Stress Tests

In this section we discuss how to source hypothetical scenarios, which we might group as systemic, idiosyncratic, or extra-financial.

One of the main challenges in generating tail scenarios are general cognitive biases, in particular overconfidence and underestimation of tail events. History shows that financial analysts and economists tend to greatly underestimate the potential range of outcomes. For example, long term studies of economist predictions have shown that 90% range estimate for GDP changes were exceeded 50% of time (read Nate Silver’s “The Signal and the Noise: Why So Many Predictions Fail — but Some Don’t” 2012). Given that the same cognitive biases befall professional managers and investors, in addition to generating tail scenarios, another challenge is to convince management that these are credible.

It helps greatly to tap into network intelligence both for idea generation and to convince management that tail scenarios are worth considering. We list some credible thought leaders (e.g., Robert Shiller, Nouriel Roubini, David Rosenberg, Ken Rogoff) and organizations which provide updated scenarios (from regulatory agencies to financial institutions and media). We also suggest using Google to tap into network intelligence around stress scenarios.

As an example, we review Byron Wien’s Ten Surprises for 2012 (and you might be interested in the more current Byron Wien’s Ten Surprises for 2013).

We also mention some potential extra financial scenarios to consider.
