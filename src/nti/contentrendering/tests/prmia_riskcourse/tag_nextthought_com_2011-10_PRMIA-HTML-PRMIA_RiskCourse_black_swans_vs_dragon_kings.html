Black Swans vs Dragon Kings

Black Swans

How predictable is systemic risk? Nassim Taleb’s “Black Swan” proposes that history is shaped by rare and unpredictable extreme events. Indeed, history is rife with Black Swans such as Eisenhower’s 1955 heart attack or the 911 terrorist attack which sent financial markets tumbling. We might call this type of Black Swan an exogenous shock (i.e., from outside the system). But other outlier events Taleb lists as Black Swans, including the rise of the Personal Computer and the Internet, and best sellers like Harry Potter, have different characteristics. Their emergence initially only cause a small ripple which slowly grows until an irreversible tipping-point of self-amplifying growth is crossed. This is a classic pattern of an endogenous shock (i.e., from inside the system) which can be pre-diagnosed and quantified to a degree.

Dragon Kings

Professor Didier Sornette is one of the leading risk researchers who focuses on the prediction of systemic risks:

“We develop the concept of ‘dragon-kings’ corresponding to meaningful outliers, which are found to coexist with power laws in the distributions of event sizes under a broad range of conditions in a large variety of systems. These dragon-kings reveal the existence of mechanisms of self-organization that are not apparent otherwise from the distribution of their smaller siblings.” (Dragon-Kings, Black Swans and the Prediction of Crises)

The first step to diagnosis of endogenous risk is to examine the systemic vulnerabilities, as we might do when assessing the risk of a forest fire or an avalanche (e.g., terrain, past weather and near term forecasts). Such top-down analysis might yield a very high risk of forest fire, avalanche, or financial crisis, and then identify micro risk factors to monitor (e.g., mountain climbers entering the danger zone). Once having assessed systemic vulnerability, we know that many micro level outliers have the potential to amplify into major systemic risks, and the key is to detect and address these early to prevent systemic collapse (e.g., put the fire department on high alert, neutralize key pathways to decouple adjacent areas, and early detection and action to extinguish small fires).

Didier Sornette’s studies reveal that around 70% of financial crises were caused by Dragon Kings, which can be pre-diagnosed and quantified to a large degree.

Historical examples of endogenous crises include the 1987 stock market crash, the 1994 Mexican Peso Crisis, the 1997 Asian Crisis, the 1998 LTCM Crisis, Brazil’s 1998-1999 currency crisis, the 2001 Dot Com bubble burst, the 2008-2009 GFC, and the current European Sovereign Debt Crisis. Each of these crises was caused by structural instabilities that were first pre-diagnosed accurately by thoughtful analysts (top-down structural analysis), and which were then followed by outlier events triggering ever greater waves of self-amplifying volatility (bottom-up signals of endogenous risk).

So when we are faced with an event, the key question is whether it’s an exogenous shock which does not trigger amplification or whether it is endogenously self amplifying.
