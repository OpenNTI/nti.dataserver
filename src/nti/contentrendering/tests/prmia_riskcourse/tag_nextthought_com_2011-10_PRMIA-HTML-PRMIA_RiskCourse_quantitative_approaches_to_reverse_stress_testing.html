Quantitative Approaches to Reverse Stress Testing

In this section we briefly review the following three quantitative methods for RST:

  1. Sensitivity Analysis based

  2. Historical Simulation based

  3. Monte Carlo Simulation based

Sensitivity Analysis

Sensitivity analysis can often be a useful starting point for considering portfolio vulnerabilities.

Sensitivity grids can be used to quickly identify large potential losses (e.g., $5mm in Equity Derivatives Portfolio Sensitivity Grid example). As a general best practice, try to identify short gamma risk, which is dangerous due to accelerating non-linear loss potential.

As a shortcoming, sensitivity analysis does not provide total portfolio based risk estimates as correlations are not factored in.

Historical Simulation

Historical Simulation is another useful approach to RST for market risk. Running historical simulations, we can quickly identify time periods that would have resulted in large portfolio losses. The challenge with historical simulation is to interpret the meaning of results. What events and market factors are responsible for the large losses? Can we identify common themes behind large losses?

A shortcoming of historical simulation is that it only considers market based scenarios with historical precedence (and hence is inappropriate for credit or operational risk).

Monte Carlo Simulation

Monte Carlo holds promise for RST because simulations can include extreme tail movements which may not have had historical precedence. Monte Carlo simulation can be useful for both market and credit risk.

As with historical simulation, we examine scenarios that produce a loss above the threshold amount. The main challenge is again to interpret loss scenarios:

  1. what are the key driving market factors that produce large losses,

  2. are there common themes behind large loss scenarios?
