Adaptive Stress Testing Framework

Macro vs Micro

We begin with a macro analysis of risk in its structural and hidden form (e.g., fault lines, risk in potential form). Examples include analysis of economic imbalances, leverage, and asset valuations.

From the micro perspective, we monitor price movements, in particular, changes in market volatility (e.g., visible or realized risk).

Macro and micro perspectives of risk continually inform each other. For example, macro analysis might lead us to monitor the movements of specific asset prices (micro). Micro level volatility and correlation spikes can then be linked to a range of potentially affected systemic fault lines (e.g., a jump in agricultural prices might lead us to consider the potential effects of Agflation scenarios).

Adaptive Stress Library (ASL)

Our ASL follows this macro-micro architecture.

  1. Macro: we first populate our scenario library with stress scenarios from Innovators (e.g., Nouriel Roubini, Robert Shiller, Ken Rogoff, David Rosenberg, etc.). Thought leaders are a source of ideas for potential systemic risk.

  2. Micro: we then use market signals to monitor the emergence of key scenarios (e.g., Early Adopters).

We apply the theory of Diffusions of Innovations as popularized by Geoffrey Moore’s Crossing the Chasm to show how the jump from Innovators to Early Adopters starts a tipping point of adoption by the Early Majority & Late Majority. The phase transition of “crossing the chasm” is characterized by outliers, super-exponential growth and a period of high volatility.

Risky Misperception: Is Volatility Risk?

A common misperception is to equate volatility with risk. Volatility only measures the visible aspect of risk, not its hidden potential form.

Periods of low volatility have historically lulled many investors into overconfidence and complacency into taking ever-larger leveraged positions that fuel the final toxic stage of asset bubbles. In other words, as Hyman Minsky has noted, stability breeds instability.

During periods of low volatility, it’s prudent to consider that a large proportion of risk may be in a hidden potential form, ready to emerge as a coiled spring (which releases potential energy into kinetic energy). Therefore, periods of low volatility are important opportunities to explore potential hidden portfolio concentrations via extensive stress testing. Periods of low volatility are also present opportunities to purchase cheap portfolio insurance (e.g., buying cheap umbrellas while it’s sunny to sell on a rainy day).

Financials “Death Star Pulse”: Hidden Risks Emerge

The 2008-2009 U.S. Subprime crisis was a classic Minsky Moment: low perceived levels of risk as measured by volatility, credit spreads, and credit ratings helped fuel unprecedented levels of leverage globally before collapsing into a deleveraging spiral.

Some Innovators, such as Robert Shiller and Nouriel Roubini, called the housing bubble early... in 2003 and 2005 respectively... years before the bubble peaked. While market timing may not be their strength, Innovators can recognize potential future risks which are hidden to most.

The “Roubini/Shiller subprime scenario” first emerged as visible risk in late Dec 2006 when AAA rated subprime bond volatility spiked, leading GS to exit the market. More outliers followed over the next six months, including a larger outlier spike that occurred in Feb 2007 after HSBC was the first bank to announce a $10.5bn subprime loss followed by other industry losses such as the default of subprime lender NEW and two Bear Stearns subprime hedge funds. Surprisingly, financials recovered from this sell-off to peak in June 2007. After that, we observe a classic endogenous risk tipping point: ever increasing volatility spikes, driving a self-amplifying deleveraging cycle.

By March 2009 the subprime scenario had run its course, and a “financial recovery” scenario emerged as volatility declined.

Do you prefer invisible or visible risk?
It’s worth noting that 2006 to early 2007 was characterized by historically low market volatility, despite all the structural risks of subprime being present (and recognized by Shiller, Roubini and like). In other words, low visible risk but high potential risk. On the other hand at the March 2009 bottom, volatility was elevated, but at least risk was largely visible as opposed to invisible. This is the logic behind the contrarian credo to “buy when there is blood in the streets.”
