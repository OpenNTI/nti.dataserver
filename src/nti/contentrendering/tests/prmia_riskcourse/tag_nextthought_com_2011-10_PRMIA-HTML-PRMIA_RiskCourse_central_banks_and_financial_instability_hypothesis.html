Central Banks and Financial Instability Hypothesis

This module discusses governance of systemic risk. Complex systems present governance challenges due to non-linear interactions of amplifiers and dampeners.

As presented in the first module, financial boom bust cycles are often preceded by spikes in international capital flows and the feedback loop between asset values and leverage. Other amplifiers include the feedback between risk appetite and asset prices, and financial markets and the real economy.

A bank run is a classical example, where perceived weakness and deposit withdrawals amplify each other into a self-fulfilling a which then cascades into a death spiral. Once a tipping point is crossed, the bank is powerless to reverse this process without external help (i.e., regulatory intervention or takeover by an institution which is perceived to be sound). Given the interconnectedness of financial institutions, a bank run can cascade into a systemic run. We witnessed this in the 2008 Lehman bankruptcy, which morphed into a global run on the financial system which required unprecedented global governmental intervention.

As an example, a bank run starts with deposit withdrawals due to perceived weakness which then cascades into a self-fulfilling death spiral, which then spreads to other financial institutions. We witnessed such a death spiral in the 2008 Lehman bankruptcy which cascaded into a global run on the financial system.

We introduce George Cooper’s “Origins of Financial Crises” (2008) as a foundational work on systemic risk and governance. Cooper’s essay extends Hyman Minsky’s Financial Instability Hypothesis, which argues that financial markets are inherently unstable. Cooper reasons that central banks therefore play a crucial role in managing systemic risk, but that their historical policy of asymmetric intervention is deeply flawed. Asymmetric intervention mitigates small crises at the cost of ever larger financial bubbles and busts. Cooper concludes by making a powerful case for loose symmetric central bank intervention. He draws on inspiration from physicist James Clerk Maxwell’s seminal 1868 paper (“On Governors”) which helped stabilize the steam engines that powered the industrial age.
