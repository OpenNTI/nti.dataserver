Polarity Management

Polarities are interdependent opposites which power all complex systems. We are immersed in a continual cycle of polarities, from each breath (in ∞ out) to the binary code (0 ∞ 1) and electrical power (+ ∞ −) which enables this course.

Common examples of polarities include

  • Individual ∞ Team

  • Centralized ∞ Decentralized

  • Standardized ∞ Customized

  • Optimized ∞ Flexible

While we have individual preferences, it’s important to note that each polarity has positive attributes worth striving for. However, each polarity also has potential downsides, and the key to their effective management is awareness of early warning signals.

The cycle of polarity management takes us from the positive attributes of a polarity (e.g., individual) to its negative, and then to the positive aspect of its opposite (e.g., team) and then its negative in a continual infinity shaped loop. We highly recommend Barry Johnson’s seminal “Polarity Management” as primer.

Complexity Drives the Need for Polarity Management

Many of the world’s most complex problems are not solvable, but rather call for more effective polarity management.

Polarity management is a crucial risk management practice. Too often, we seek to find solutions (e.g., more accurate statistical calculators) when the more important challenge is to better manage polarities (e.g., how to manage top-down vs bottom-up perspectives of risk).

As related to risk management, these are some major polarities to consider

  1. Visible risk ∞ Invisible risk

  2. Centralized risk management ∞ decentralized risk taking

  3. Short Term ∞ Long Term

  4. Tactical ∞ Strategic

  5. Macro ∞ Micro

  6. Focus on Profitability ∞ Focus on Risk

Risky Misperceptions: The Danger of One Integrated Risk Statistic

Core challenges in risk management include considering short term and long term risk, and risk under normal and stressed conditions. There is a desire, however, to “solve the risk problem” as one single purpose risk statistic. For example, the history of VaR holds some valuable lessons of uses and misuses of integrated risk statistics.

VaR rose to prominence as a unified portfolio trading risk metric. It was popularized in the early 1990’s by JPMorgan’s 4.15pm risk report, which estimated Daily Earnings At Risk (DEaR) at 95% confidence across all trading positions globally. JPMorgan’s CEO Dennis Weatherstone showed a keen understanding of polarities in his response to why he chose the 95% confidence level for VaR: “I trust our models 95%, but pay my risk managers well to look after the remaining 5%.”

As VaR proved a useful market risk metric, Basle approved it for trading risk regulatory capital. Regulations specified a 99% confidence interval over a 10 day horizon, and the use of one year or more of data. But some banks calculated VaR with 5 years of evenly weighted data, believing that more data can only help. Furthermore, penalty factors for excessive VaR outliers encouraged some banks to build in opaque layers of conservative assumptions that made excessions rare. For example, despite an expectation of 2-3 outliers per year, UBS had no single VaR outlier from 1998 for 9 years until it recorded its first outlier in 2007. But as the subprime crisis escalated, these models were slow to adapt. UBS recorded 50 outliers in 2008 alone, when firms like Bank of America, which switched a more adaptive model, recorded 2. What seemed like a conservative assumption at first turned out to be the opposite. A stable and seemingly high VaR estimate led to complacency and slow recognition of escalating risk..

Instead of mixing polarities of short term vs long term and normal vs extreme risk measurement into one integrated statistics, investors are better served by using different approaches for each polarity, as Sir Weatherstone hinted at. Responsive VaR models (e.g., RiskMetrics) performed very well at the 95% confidence level as a short term risk measure, and even provided useful early warning signals as subprime risk escalated. On the other hand, stress testing (by fairly compensated risk managers!) is more appropriate for considering tail risks and longer term risk scenarios. Effective polarity management requires a dynamic understanding of what is appropriate in each situation, and avoiding the temptation for one universal solution.
