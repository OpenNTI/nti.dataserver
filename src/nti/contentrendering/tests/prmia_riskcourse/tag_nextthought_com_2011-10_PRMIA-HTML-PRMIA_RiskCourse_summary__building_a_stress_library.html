Summary: Building A Stress Library

In summary, build a rich and flexible stress libary and practice the following:

  1. Enrich scenarios with multiple tags to view risks in many dimensions. For example, highlight common driving factors and themes. For example, the scneario “EUR inflationary bust” might include the following tags: EUR, inflationary, deleveraging, sovereign risk, credit risk, recession.

  2. Rather than implement a library of fixed scenarios, continually source new scenarios from thought leaders. These scenarios must be translated into key driving market factors, loss severity, and time frame (e.g., 1 week, French financials down 25%, French CDS +100bp), given a meaningful name (e.g., “Mauldin French financials meltdown”), and tagged appropriately (e.g., France, financials, credit risk, sovereign risk, deleveraging).

  3. Beyond static risk reporting, engage with scenarios to better understand feedback loops over time. War games allow management to (a) explore how events could affect decisionmaking, (b) identify weaknesses and opportunities, and (c) prepare for live crisis situations. Given how stressful situations impair cognition and the ability to collaborate, practice is a prerequisite to master stressful events.

  4. Finally, develop the ability to effectively monitor and respond to emerging scenarios before tipping points are crossed. And effective “stakeout” ideally involves designing indices that incorporate the driving factors of each scenario. Establish a discipline of daily monitoring, and engage in discussion when pre-determined triggers are breached or outliers are observed. For market risks, an outlier threshold level of 95% daily confidence level is a useful trigger point for discussion. This will get a firm in the practice of discussing systemic risks on average once every 20 trading days... and much more as crisis situations unfold. This discipline therefore serves both as a discipline for mastery and potentially early warning.
