Goldman Sachs Risk Management Case Study

This module discusses Goldman Sachs (GS) risk management, including: risk methodologies; reports; culture; strengths and weaknesses; risk appetite and loss tolerance; monitoring VaR outliers as early warning; risk concentrations; key market and credit risk model limitations such as wrong-way-risk and crowded trades.

Summary
Goldman Sachs (GS) is a preeminent investment firm known both for its aggressive pursuit of profits and its disciplined internal risk management. While it was one of only a few large firms who managed to stay profitable through the GFC, its reputation of acting in the best interest of clients was sullied by structuring and selling subprime investments long after it had exited the market. In this case study we examine GS’s approach to risk management, and point out both strengths and weaknesses.

Collaborative risk culture with enterprise view
GS’s disciplined risk culture starts at the top. Senior managers actively participate in enterprise risk discussions, including scenario generation and firm-wide stress testing. Senior executives (including CEO and CFO) often roam trading floors and ask detailed questions about market conditions. GS has been an early adopter of Reverse Stress Tests to explore firmwide risk concentrations. The firm seeks to manage enterprise risk by aggregating market, credit, liquidity, and operational risks across all its business lines globally.

GS discusses its internal risk models and loss tolerance
GS shared useful insights about how it manages risk at a conference it hosted during the GFC. A senior manager presented the layout of internal risk reports. Reports included VaR and stress tests to highlight potential loss concentrations due to market shocks, with an emphasis on country & name concentration. A derivatives trader noted how GS’s pain threshold for individual losses can be high as long as management is convinced that the trade makes sense and does not contribute to excessive firmwide concentration risk. On the other hand, there is little tolerance for any investments that contribute to unwanted risk concentrations (e.g., subprime securities starting in Dec 2006).

Emphasis on market valuations, not models
GS managers readily admit that their risk models are imperfect and perhaps not even the best in the industry. Instead, they emphasize their practical use of models, with an acute awareness of limitations. They actively monitor market valuations, and hone in on situations where price fluctuations seem to contradict internal risk or valuation models. This was in contrast to a number of firms who resisted market price proxies and emphasized internal valuation models (these were also the firms that experienced the greatest subprime losses).

Active daily monitoring of outliers
This market discipline allows GS to quickly adapt to abnormal conditions. For example, GS monitors daily P&L vs VaR, and takes outliers as potential early warning signals. As soon as a risk limit is breached or an outlier is observed, traders and risk managers engage in discussions, bringing in expertise from various parts of the firm as needed. For example, management noticed unusual clustering of VaR excessions over a period of 9 days in Dec 2006. Despite realized losses being very small at the time, they realized that subprime losses could be very substantial; senior management decided to exit all subprime investments. In continually testing their models against the market, they transformed a model limitation (poor tail risk estimation) into an advantage (as early warning information). For a more extensive discussion of uses and misuses of risk models, see “Forecasting underperformance: looking for risk in all the right places.”
