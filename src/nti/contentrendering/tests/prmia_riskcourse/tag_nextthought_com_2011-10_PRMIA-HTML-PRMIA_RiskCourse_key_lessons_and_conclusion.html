Key Lessons and Conclusion

Ecosystem View

Our first recommendation is to take an ecosystem view, and to focus on feedback mechanisms.

Many risk reduction techniques are not what they seem. The initial reduction of individual risk comes back with interest in the form of amplified systemic risk. Classic examples include:

  1. Hoarding of liquidity during stress events deepens liquidity crises

  2. Stop loss (and fixed VaR limits) can amplify market moves

  3. Portfolio insurance through delta hedged equity portfolios were a major cause of the 1987 stock market crash

  4. The synchronized rush to hedge foreign liabilities precipitated the Asian crisis

  5. Deleveraging can lead to a self-amplifying death spiral (e.g., GFC)

  6. Fire-sale of collateral can trigger a broader collapse (e.g., see ML’s attempted sale of subprime collateral from Bear’s hedge fund)

The above are all false safety systems. They work at small scale, but amplify systemic risk during crises. When considering risk mitigation, it’s imperative to consider feedback loops. The fundamental question is: What signal am I sending to the marketplace through my action, and what would happen if others followed suit?

It helps to focus on key nodes of the system when doing ecosystem risk analysis:

  • Professor Darrel Duffie’s 10 by 10 by 10 proposal is gaining traction for systemic stress testing by focusing on the center of the network (top 10 financial institutions, top 10 counterparty exposures, and top 10 scenarios).

  • Network visualizations can also help identify core nodes in the system, and highlight likely vulnerabilities.

Proactive Risk Management Uncovers Hidden Risks... Before They Emerge as Visible

We have greatest control when we uncover risk in potential form, before tipping points are crossed. As outlined in the Adaptive Stress Testing module, we start with a macro view of structural imbalances (e.g., asset bubbles, overvaluation, leveraged bets by poorly informed market participants, misaligned incentives). Then we take the micro perspective, and first consider trends, with a view of how sustainable they are. Trends are a great starting point to identify crowded trades. Outliers and escalating volatility are signals of impending regime shifts. As we saw in the subprime crisis case study, these initial outliers were observed many months before the crisis tipping point occurred, leaving time for orderly exits and other risk strategies such as increasing liquidity and capital buffers. The more people leave the theatre early, the less crowded and vulnerable to runs it will be.

Consider Countercyclical and Contrarian Strategies

“The biggest pitfall in investments is herd behavior. Large gains in performance can be achieved by investors with ability to consistently act contrarian” Knut N Kjaer, “New Ideas on Future Portfolio Design”, 4 May 2011

Trend following strategies are the most common, and for nimble institutions these can be profitable, although individual investors are often the last of the pack to exit the strategy. The systemic downside of trend following is that it amplifies market cycles. Countercyclical strategies tend to be profitable at longer time frames, and they have the benefit of dampening systemic volatility. Some examples of successful countercyclical strategies include:

  • Yales’s CIO David Swensen uses daily rebalancing of his portfolio as a contrarian strategy which reaps long term rewards. To keep his target asset allocation, he sells when the price goes up and buys when the price goes down. In the long run, this service of providing liquidity to the marketplace will be rewarded with positive returns.

  • Volatility can be a useful contrarian trading strategy, given its tendency towards mean reversion. Over the last 100 years, broad US equity market volatility has averaged between 15-20%. A strategy of buying volatility when it dips below the average and selling volatility during crisis situations can be profitable while dampening systemic risk.

  • Tail risk strategies can also be profitable in the long run and offer valuable protection when standard asset classes are suffering. Tail risk strategies typically involve purchasing undervalued options, with the expectation of fat tailed events.

  • Research has shown that buying equities when valuation is low tends to generate superior long term returns (e.g., see Antti Ilmanen’s Expected Returns: An Investor’s Guide to Harvesting Market Rewards).

Decouple Risks

“The usual response to complexity, that of trial and error, is not enough when faced with systems which are not only complex, but also tightly coupled. The costs of error are simply too high. The instinctive answer is to eliminate the errors. This is an impossible dream. The alternative is to try to simplify and to decouple these high-risk systems as much as is feasible, to encourage whistleblowers to identify latent errors waiting to strike, and - sadly - to stand prepared for the worst.” Harford, Tim (2011). Adapt: Why Success Always Starts with Failure

One of the dark sides of globalization is highly coupled risks. When possible, we must try to decouple these risks as much as possible, for example:

  • Discourage homogeneity and monoculture in favor of diversification (from global food supply to energy to financial services).

  • Small is beautiful: split big bets and big projects into many smaller experiments where we can learn from failure and success, similar to venture capital style investment.

  • Instead of attempting to minimize failures, focus on network resilience. Google’s data centers are a great case study. The system consists of many cheap components with high failure rates, but the overall system has been designed to be highly resilient.

  • Encourage bottom up ownership and management of risk when possible in order to maximize learning at every level of the organization and reduce the risk of top down failures.

For systems which are difficult to decouple and where the consequence of failure is severe as in nuclear power plants, we need to focus efforts on continually stress testing for hidden vulnerabilities and immediately responding to emerging risks to mitigate the worst effects.

Avalanche Risk

The slide below shows Mont Maudit, where 9 climbers died from a 50 foot avalanche in July 2012.

The avalanche analogy holds some important risk lessons

  1. Start by analyzing structural (potential) risk. In this case, rising temperatures had raised avalanche risk, which led some prudent climbers to abandon their climbs.

  2. In the event that we are on the mountain, extreme vigilance is called for as even the smallest perturbation can lead to cascading failure.

  3. In the event of a perturbation, quick immediate reaction can be the difference between life and death, as can rapid response by rescue crew.

Risk Management is a Common Good

Interconnectedness has the potential to turn any risk management failure into a risk avalanche. Systemic risks transcend national borders and will challenge our current national governance structures.

Mass collaboration platforms offer the most promising new ways of responding to emerging systemic threats. A good case study was the 2010 Haiti Earthquake, where Google saved lives using Google Maps and other online resources.

But rather than waiting for disaster to strike, we can be more proactive about identifying fault lines before they erupt. As CBS news put it, “Haiti was a disaster waiting to happen.” Seismologists had warned Haiti about the risk on that very fault line years earlier, and yet there was no preparation, and no improvement in infrastructure or build quality. The earthquake was not preventable, but stress testing had identified vulnerabilities that could have been mitigated to save many lives.

Scenario based analysis will be crucial for better understanding systemic risks while still in their potential form, and while there is still a level of control. Stress tests can help us identify and strengthen key nodes in our networks, and be better prepared for breakdowns once they occur.

Spark Network Intelligence

We live in an era of networked intelligence facilitated by social technologies. The risk community could benefit greatly by adopting more social technologies to better collaborate and share information. A common stress testing platform offers great promise, both for sharing and evaluation of stress scenarios and early warning signals. We hope the social technologies offered by NextThought in this course will start the process of connecting a global community of like minded risk managers.

Summary and Conclusion

We live in an era of ever accelerating complexity which challenges us to make an evolutionary leap in risk management.

We know that mopping up the mess afterwards is NOT an option. We will manage risks proactively. We will apply stress tests to identify hidden vulnerabilities before they become emergencies. And we will integrate market intelligence with scenario analysis to respond quickly to escalating patterns of systemic risk before tipping points are crossed.

We recognize the dangers of coupled risks. We will take an ecosystem view, consider feedback loops, and encourage diversification and decoupling at all levels: from contrarian investment strategies to countercyclical capital allocation and monetary policy.

And we know that we’re not in this alone. We’ll use social platforms to share information and spark network intelligence. We’ve evolved from siloed risk management to enterprise risk management. Next up: global risk community using social collaboration platforms.
